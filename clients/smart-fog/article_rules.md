--- SMART FOG VOICE AND STYLE RULES ---

Follow these rules exactly when writing every sentence.

**Person and audience:**
- Write in third person throughout. The subject is always "facilities," "manufacturers," "operators," "engineers," "the system," "the technology." Never use "you" or "your" in body copy.
- Exception: FAQ answers may use second person when answering a direct how/what/why question from the reader's perspective, but keep it minimal.
- Assume the reader is a facility engineer, plant manager, or procurement decision-maker with technical knowledge. Never over-explain basics that an industrial professional would know. Never talk down to the reader.

**Mechanism before benefit:**
- Always lead with the technical mechanism or physics, then state the benefit. Never lead with the benefit and back-fill the explanation.
- Correct: "Smart Fog systems use compressed air and water through a proprietary nozzle to produce self-evaporating droplets. This prevents surface wetting while maintaining stable humidity."
- Wrong: "Smart Fog keeps surfaces dry. It does this using compressed air and water."

**Claim qualification:**
- Performance claims must always be appropriately qualified: "under proper system design," "designed to," "helps maintain," "engineered for."
- Non-wetting is a guarantee but carries a caveat: "non-wetting" applies to surfaces under proper system design; direct exposure to the fog stream will wet surfaces. Include this caveat wherever non-wetting is strongly claimed.
- Never state specific energy savings percentages, ROI multiples, or productivity improvements without citing a specific Smart Fog case study or verified third-party source.

**Intro discipline:**
- The intro must open with a claim, a decision-relevant tension, or a direct answer to the primary search query. Never open with a sentence that describes the article's structure.
- Forbidden intro patterns: "This article covers...", "In this guide, we will explore...", "This comprehensive comparison analyzes...", "This guide examines... The analysis covers...". These are AI throat-clearing and signal generated content to both readers and LLMs.
- The first sentence must make an argument or establish a tension. The second sentence states the scope. Structure-describing prose belongs nowhere in the article.

**Sentence and paragraph structure:**
- Mix short declarative sentences with slightly longer explanatory ones. Avoid paragraphs where every sentence is the same length or structure.
- Paragraphs must be 2-4 sentences. Never write a paragraph longer than 4 sentences. Never write a single-sentence paragraph except for deliberate structural emphasis, used sparingly.
- The intro and conclusion must each be broken into separate paragraphs of no more than 3-4 sentences. A wall of text spanning 5+ sentences with no break is a formatting failure.
- Each paragraph must connect logically to the one before it. Never write consecutive paragraphs that each make isolated standalone points with no logical connection.
- Sections should be 3-5 paragraphs, not counting any bullets within them.
- Opening paragraphs for H2 and H3 sections must establish WHY the dimension matters for the reader's decision — not preview or restate the data that bullet clusters or lists in the same section will deliver. If the opening paragraph and the bullet cluster below it make the same claim, delete the prose. Every factual claim must appear once. Writing it in a setup paragraph and then again as a bullet is redundancy, not depth.

**Mechanism section word discipline:**
- Sections that explain how a technology works must be capped at 3-4 sentences of mechanism explanation. Establish the operating principle and move on. The reader is a facility engineer — they do not need a textbook explanation.
- The value of any comparison or application article is in the trade-offs, selection criteria, and application specificity — not in re-explaining mechanisms the reader already understands. If mechanism explanation is running long, cut it, not the comparison content.

**Industry application specificity:**
- Any section that names a specific industry or use case must include at least one concrete specification or threshold relevant to that industry: a required humidity range, a temperature constraint, a regulatory standard, or a failure-mode threshold. One number per use case is the minimum.
- Every such specification must be backed by a verified external citation embedded at the point of the claim. An industry application section with no cited data point is not complete.
- Do not state that a technology "works well for" an industry without naming the specific condition that makes it appropriate. Assertions without specifications are not useful to the reader.

**Prohibited words and phrases:**
- No em dashes anywhere. Use a comma or restructure the sentence.
- Never use: "it's worth noting," "it's important to understand," "in conclusion," "furthermore," "moreover," "delve," "comprehensive," "crucial," "showcasing," "let's explore," "game-changer," "multifaceted," "nuanced," "key takeaway," "it is essential," "leveraging," "robust."
- Oxford comma required in all lists: "a, b, and c."
- Spell out abbreviations on first use: "relative humidity (RH)," "electrostatic discharge (ESD)," "heating, ventilation, and air conditioning (HVAC)."

**Formatting:**
- Always open with a Key Takeaways section immediately after the intro (before the first H2). Format it as a proper bullet list of 4-6 items. Each bullet is a complete, assertive, standalone sentence that states a specific technical fact or differentiator — not a conclusion or summary assertion. A Key Takeaway must be falsifiable: it should contain a concrete specification, threshold, quantified claim, or named mechanism. Wrong: "Installation complexity and energy consumption make dry fog the most cost-effective solution." Right: "Dry fog systems require no certified technician for installation and operate on existing compressed air infrastructure, eliminating the dedicated electrical circuits and ductwork modifications that steam requires." If a bullet cannot be supported with a specific fact from the article, rewrite it until it can.
- NO TABLES — Smart Fog's WordPress does not render HTML or markdown tables correctly. Never use table syntax. If the brief specifies a table, convert it to a bullet cluster or structured paragraphs as described below.
- Comparison formatting rule (applies to every comparison of 3+ items across attributes — technology types, equipment categories, industry sectors, or any multi-item comparison the brief or table would have shown): use a bullet cluster. A bullet cluster groups all compared items under a single named comparison dimension. Format each bullet as: **Item Name:** one sentence. The bold item name and colon are mandatory on every single bullet — no item may ever appear without **bold**: format. Use a colon after the bold name — not a hyphen, not an em dash. All compared items must appear in every cluster. One sentence per bullet — two sentences makes it a paragraph with a bullet point, not a scannable comparison. The comparison dimensions (energy, precision, surface wetting, maintenance cost, etc.) must be established at the start of the section — do not let them vary mid-article. For 2-item comparisons, use structured paragraphs instead of bullet clusters.
- When the brief specifies comparison criteria or attributes, use exactly those as the cluster dimensions — do not substitute or invent new ones.
- Use bullet lists for 3+ unordered factors, symptoms, causes, or items.
- Use numbered lists for sequential steps or ranked processes.
- Use paragraphs for explanations and narrative where ideas build on each other.
- Every H2 and H3 section must open with at least one full body paragraph before any bullet list or numbered list. Never place a list as the first element after a heading.
- When the brief specifies a format for a section, follow it exactly — except replace any specified table with a bullet list.

--- SMART FOG BRAND PLACEMENT RULES ---

**When Smart Fog appears:**
Smart Fog must appear as the second-to-last H2, immediately before the Final Thoughts or Summary section. The first half of every article is entirely client-agnostic. Smart Fog enters late and earns its place through the article's technical argument.

**Smart Fog section structure:**
The Smart Fog section must contain at least two H3s. Structure them as:
1. First H3: mechanism or specification angle -- how Smart Fog's technology addresses the specific challenge the article covers
2. Second H3: outcomes or facility benefits -- real-world performance in the context of the article's industry or use case

**Smart Fog section tone:**
- The Smart Fog section must NOT open with brand-first language. Do not open with "Smart Fog systems address...", "Smart Fog delivers...", or "Smart Fog's technology provides...". These are assertions about the brand, not technical claims.
- Open with a technology claim that describes what the mechanism does. The brand earns its place through the technical argument, not through assertion. The reader should arrive at Smart Fog as the logical conclusion of the section, not as its starting premise.
- Wrong: "Smart Fog systems address the precision and reliability requirements described in this comparison through a proprietary adiabatic process."
- Right: "Adiabatic humidification that produces an equal-sized droplet grid eliminates the surface-wetting risk that defines both steam and ultrasonic technologies. This is the operating principle behind Smart Fog's industrial systems."
- Lead with the mechanism relevant to the article topic, then the performance benefit, then the qualification.
- Never write a generic Smart Fog overview — every Smart Fog section must be tied specifically to the article's topic.
- Use bullet lists within the Smart Fog H3s to summarise key performance points — these support AI citation and featured snippets.

**Technical accuracy rules for Smart Fog copy:**
- Use "self-evaporating droplets" or "equal-sized droplet grid" -- never state a specific droplet size in microns
- Never name competing brands or make direct comparisons by name
- Technology-type comparisons (adiabatic vs. steam vs. ultrasonic) are only acceptable in MOFU and BOFU articles
- Never claim Smart Fog dehumidifies, sterilises, or eliminates pathogens

--- LINKING RULES ---

**Internal links:**
- Embed every internal link specified in the brief using this exact format: [HYPERLINK: anchor text | full smartfog.com url]
- Do NOT use markdown link syntax [anchor text](url). The pipeline only renders [HYPERLINK: anchor text | url] format.
- Anchor text must accurately describe the destination page topic. Verify: does the anchor phrase describe what the linked page is actually about?
- Find an existing sentence that discusses the same topic as the destination and embed the link mid-sentence on a natural phrase. Never start a sentence with the linked phrase. Never write a new sentence around the link.
- Do not place internal links in the Final Thoughts or conclusion section. Links belong in body sections and FAQ answers.

**Cluster-based internal linking discipline:**
- Each article belongs to a content cluster with a designated primary landing page (specified in the brief). That landing page is the priority internal link for this article.
- Link to other industry landing pages ONLY if a section of the article directly discusses that industry and the link is genuinely useful to the reader — not simply because the page exists in the link pool.
- Linking to product and solutions pages (e.g., ES100, TS100, humidity-control-systems, esd-control-systems) is acceptable anywhere they are topically relevant — these are not cluster-restricted.
- When in doubt between linking to the cluster landing page vs. a different industry page, always choose the cluster landing page.

**External links:**
- Include 2-3 external links to authoritative sources embedded mid-sentence on specific factual claims.
- Acceptable sources: OSHA (osha.gov), EPA (epa.gov), NIOSH (niosh.cdc.gov), CDC (cdc.gov), ASHRAE (ashrae.org), NASA (nasa.gov), PubMed (pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov), PMC (pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov), NIH (nih.gov), ISO (iso.org), IEEE (ieee.org), NFPA (nfpa.org).
- Never link to competitor domains.
- External links must point to a specific page, article, or standard -- never to a bare domain homepage.
- If the specific URL cannot be confirmed, use [HYPERLINK: anchor text | UNCONFIRMED]. Never write [URL REQUIRED].
- Anchor text for external links must identify the specific claim or study: "[year if known] + [key finding or standard being cited]." Vague anchors like "research suggests" or "studies show" are not acceptable.
- Omitting external links entirely is a critical failure.

--- FUNNEL-DEPENDENT CONTENT RULES ---

**TOFU (informational):**
- Audience assumed to be researching the topic -- may not know Smart Fog or be in active buying mode
- No technology comparisons by brand name. Technology-type comparisons (adiabatic vs. steam) are not appropriate in pure TOFU content.
- Smart Fog section focuses on how the technology addresses the informational topic -- not a product pitch
- Tone: educational, mechanism-first, accessible to a technically literate but non-specialist reader

**MOFU (consideration):**
- Audience is evaluating options and comparing approaches
- Technology-type comparisons are appropriate and expected
- Smart Fog section can position more directly against categories of competing technology (never against named brands)
- Include selection criteria, evaluation frameworks, and trade-offs
- Tone: analytical, criteria-driven, assumes a reader actively building a shortlist

**BOFU (transactional/commercial):**
- Audience is close to a purchase or consultation decision
- Smart Fog section can be more direct about capabilities, configurations, and next steps
- Include a specific CTA tied to the article's industry or application
- Tone: precise, specification-focused, action-oriented

--- FAQ RULES ---

- FAQ answers must be written as completely self-contained answers. Each answer must stand alone without any context from the article body.
- Required structure for every FAQ answer: (1) re-establish the subject being answered in one phrase, (2) state the direct answer, (3) give the key differentiator or qualification. Maximum 3-4 sentences per answer.
- Never write an FAQ answer that assumes the reader has read the article. FAQ answers are pulled as standalone snippets by Google PAA, AI Overviews, and LLMs — they must make sense in complete isolation.
- Never use "as discussed above," "as mentioned earlier," or any back-reference to the article body. Each FAQ answer is a fresh, independent response.
- FAQ answers may use second person ("your facility," "you need") where it aids directness.

--- CTA RULES ---

- All CTAs that invite the reader to contact Smart Fog, speak to an engineer, request a quote, or request a facility assessment MUST use this exact URL: https://www.smartfog.com/contact-us/
- Never link a contact/consultation CTA to any other page — not a product page, not an industry landing page, not the homepage.
- The CTA must close with a specific, low-friction next step that matches where a mid-funnel reader actually is. "Contact Smart Fog to discuss humidification" is too vague and is a failure.
- Required CTA patterns — choose the most appropriate and fill in the specific application:
  - "Request a system assessment for [specific facility type or application]" → [HYPERLINK: request a system assessment | https://www.smartfog.com/contact-us/]
  - "Speak with a Smart Fog engineer about humidification requirements for [industry]" → [HYPERLINK: speak with a Smart Fog engineer | https://www.smartfog.com/contact-us/]
  - "Get a humidification specification review for [facility type]" → [HYPERLINK: get a specification review | https://www.smartfog.com/contact-us/]
- The action named in the CTA (assessment, spec review, engineering consultation) must be specific enough that a reader knows exactly what they are requesting. Generic invitations to "contact us" are not acceptable.
- Place the CTA at the end of the Final Thoughts section, before the FAQ.
