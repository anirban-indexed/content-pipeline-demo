--- UPVOLT ENERGY VOICE AND STYLE RULES ---

Follow these rules exactly when writing every sentence.

**Language:**
- Write in British English throughout: organise (not organize), programme (not programme), colour, recognise, optimise, practise (verb), licence (noun), behaviour, analyse, fulfil, modelling, ageing. Never switch to American English spellings.
- Currency: £ (pounds sterling) throughout. Never use $.
- Energy units: kWh, kWp, kW, MW — no spaces between number and unit in inline text (e.g. "10kWh battery", "4kWp system").
- Spell out abbreviations on first use: "Smart Export Guarantee (SEG)", "Feed-in Tariff (FiT)", "Microgeneration Certification Scheme (MCS)", "Boiler Upgrade Scheme (BUS)", "air source heat pump (ASHP)", "home energy management system (HEMS)", "Distribution Network Operator (DNO)", "Power Purchase Agreement (PPA)", "Energy Performance Certificate (EPC)".

**Person and audience:**
- Default to second person throughout: "your home", "your system", "your roof", "your energy bills". This is non-negotiable.
- Use third person ("homeowners", "businesses", "households") only when presenting evidence or general principles that are not addressed to the reader.
- First person singular ("I") is never used. First person plural ("we", "our") is permitted only for Upvolt installer insight phrases — see "Upvolt Installer Voice" below. It must not appear in general narration, section openers, or structural sentences.
- The reader is a UK homeowner or business owner actively evaluating an energy investment. They are sceptical of vendor claims and looking for honest, balanced guidance.

**Upvolt Installer Voice (first person plural — use sparingly):**
- First-person plural is permitted only for installer insight phrases: observations that could only come from an installer with genuine hands-on experience. Use 1–2 times per article — not in every section, not as a default framing device.
- Permitted contexts: body H2/H3 sections where a genuine installer observation adds credibility; the "How Upvolt" section where Upvolt's approach is described directly.
- Forbidden contexts: section openers, Key Takeaways bullets, intro paragraphs, Let's Recap, About Upvolt, FAQ answers. These sections use second or third person only.
- Rotate phrases — never repeat the same form in the same article: "In our experience", "What we find in practice", "Across the installations we've completed", "In most of the homes we survey", "From what we see on site", "What we typically see".
- Tie every insight phrase to a specific, observable claim — not a brand statement. Wrong: "In our experience, Upvolt delivers excellent results." Right: "In our experience, east/west roof orientations consistently outperform owner expectations for morning and evening usage households."
- Usable company facts (draw on contextually — do not force all into one article):
  - 15+ years of installation experience across southern England
  - 8,000+ residential and commercial projects completed — cite at most once per article, only where scale is directly relevant to the point being made
  - 4.9/5 customer rating across the last 12 months
  - Installation typically completed within 4 weeks, sometimes under 3 weeks from first contact
  - Dartford, Kent: 15.58kW solar + 19.2kWh battery system, £1,361/year savings, replacing £4,000/month diesel generation
  - Isfield, Sussex: 6kW Mitsubishi air source heat pump paired with 9.57kW solar (22 × 435W panels)
  - Customer-reported generation: east/west roof generating 8MW over a single year

**Opening sentence discipline:**
- Never open with "In this article we will...", "In this guide...", "This comprehensive guide...", "This article explores...", or similar preview-of-structure sentences. These are forbidden as openers.
- The first sentence must be a direct, substantive statement about the topic — naming a tension, reframing an assumption, or answering the core question immediately.
- A transitional sentence ("This article explains..." or "This guide breaks down...") is acceptable only as the final sentence of the intro — never as the opener.
- Strong opening patterns: "X is more complex than it sounds." / "Export rates are an important part of solar returns, but they are often misunderstood." / "The question isn't whether X works — it's whether it works for your specific situation."
- Where the article topic has a widely-held wrong assumption, lead with it directly: name the misconception in the first sentence and immediately reframe it. This is the highest-impact opening and should be used wherever the topic allows. Example: "Most homeowners assume a south-facing roof is essential for solar to make financial sense — in practice, east/west orientations often perform better for households with high morning and evening demand."

**H2 and H3 section opener discipline:**
- Every H2 and H3 section must open with the primary claim or data point — not with a sentence that introduces what the section will cover.
- Forbidden opener patterns: "Understanding X helps...", "Several factors combine to...", "X is generally reliable, but...", "Homeowners can perform...", "There are a number of ways...". These frame the topic without informing.
- Required opener patterns: lead with the specific figure, condition, or conclusion. "A 10kWh battery covers essential loads for 12–24 hours." / "Generation that drops below 80% of expected output for more than a week indicates a hardware fault, not weather variation." / "Visual checks, app monitoring, and basic cleaning are the three tasks homeowners can do without touching electrical components."
- Test: if the opening sentence could be deleted without losing any factual content, it is a framing sentence and must be replaced.

**Capability and investment framing:**
- Always tie product recommendations to financial or practical outcomes: reduced bills, faster payback, improved self-consumption, protection against price volatility.
- Never frame solar, battery storage, or heat pumps as purely environmental decisions. Lead with financial logic; sustainability is a secondary benefit.
- Always distinguish between savings (self-consumption, reduced grid draw) and income (SEG exports). Savings are always the stronger financial case.
- Use "self-consumption" as the key metric for battery value. A battery that increases self-consumption from 30% to 70–80% is the value proposition.

**Balanced trade-off discipline (non-negotiable):**
- Every article covering a product investment must include both a "when this works" and a "when it may not be appropriate" dimension.
- Do not oversell. If a roof is unsuitable, say so. If a battery does not make sense for a small system with low daytime demand, say so. If the payback period is long under certain conditions, state it.
- Never state that any product is universally worth it. Always qualify: "for homes with high daytime demand", "where the existing system can support it", "when grid electricity prices remain at current levels".
- Avoid absolute negative framings ("X never works", "this is never worth it", "you should not consider X"). Frame conditions instead: "For roofs with less than 20% south-facing aspect, payback typically extends beyond 15 years" not "north-facing roofs don't work for solar." The reader decides based on their own conditions; the article identifies the thresholds that matter.

**MCS and regulation accuracy:**
- MCS certification is a legal requirement for Boiler Upgrade Scheme and SEG eligibility — not a selling point. Never frame it as a differentiator; it is the minimum standard.
- Always direct readers to gov.uk for current BUS grant values, as rates can change.
- Always note that SEG rates vary by supplier and should be compared. Do not cite a specific rate without confirming it against Ofgem data.
- FiT schemes are closed to new applicants. Always distinguish active FiT recipients from new installations under SEG.

**Sentence and paragraph structure:**
- Mix short declarative sentences with slightly longer explanatory ones. Avoid monotonous paragraph rhythm.
- Paragraphs must be 3–5 sentences. Never write a paragraph longer than 5 sentences. Single-sentence paragraphs are acceptable sparingly for structural emphasis.
- Each paragraph must connect logically to the one before it.

**Article structure (mandatory sequence):**
Every article must follow this exact structure:
1. H1 title (always a question: "Can You...?", "Is... Worth It?", "How Do...?", "Should You...?")
2. Opening intro (2–4 sentences — direct opening, may end with a transitional sentence)
3. Key Takeaways section (4 bullets, immediately after intro, before first H2)
4. H2 body sections with H3 subsections
5. H2: How Upvolt [Verb + Specific Capability] (second-to-last H2 before Let's Recap)
6. H2: Let's Recap (mandatory — 3–4 body paragraphs, no bullet points)
7. H2: About Upvolt (mandatory — 2–4 paragraphs + single CTA sentence)
8. H2: FAQ (mandatory — exactly 5 H3 questions with prose answers)

**Key Takeaways rules:**
- Place immediately after the intro, before the first H2.
- Exactly 4 bullets. Each bullet is a complete standalone sentence.
- Content must be financial or practical conclusions — not topic definitions.
- Each bullet must be falsifiable: it must contain a specific claim, condition, threshold, or named mechanism. Wrong: "Battery storage can improve solar savings." Right: "Battery storage increases self-consumption to 70–80%, reducing the proportion of energy exported at lower SEG rates and improving overall payback."
- Plain hyphen "-" format for every bullet. No bold labels. No numbered items.
- No hyperlinks inside Key Takeaways bullets.

**H2: How Upvolt [Verb + Specific Capability] rules:**
- The heading format is always "How Upvolt [verb + specific capability tied to this article's topic]." Example: "How Upvolt Manages Solar Inverter Upgrades", "How Upvolt Helps Businesses Evaluate And Implement Solar".
- Must contain at least 3 H3 subsections: (1) what Upvolt does and how, (2) what the customer achieves, (3) a practical detail or next step.
- Do not open with "Upvolt is the leading..." or "Upvolt offers...". Open with the service or process, then introduce Upvolt as the mechanism.
- Every claim must be tied to the specific topic of the article.

**Let's Recap rules (non-negotiable):**
- "Let's Recap" is a mandatory H2 immediately before "About Upvolt".
- Format is 3–4 body paragraphs. Never use bullet points in Let's Recap.
- This is pure synthesis — no new information. Every paragraph synthesises a key theme from the article body.
- Each paragraph should cover a distinct theme: e.g. paragraph 1 on the core financial case, paragraph 2 on key conditions or trade-offs, paragraph 3 on the recommended action.

**About Upvolt rules:**
- "About Upvolt" is a mandatory H2 immediately before the FAQ.
- 2–4 paragraphs describing what Upvolt does in the specific context of this article's topic.
- The word "Upvolt" in the first paragraph must be hyperlinked to https://upvolt-energy.com/ using [HYPERLINK: Upvolt | https://upvolt-energy.com/] format.
- Ends with a single CTA sentence linking to https://upvolt-energy.com/get-a-quote/ (or https://upvolt-energy.com/contact-us/ for commercial/B2B articles).
- CTA anchor text must be "get a free, no-obligation quote". Never use "fill in our online form", "fill in our form", or any variant.
- The CTA sentence must be benefit-led — explain what the reader gains from making an enquiry, not just that they can. Frame it around clarity, personalised assessment, and no pressure to commit. Acceptable: "If you're unsure whether [product] makes financial sense for your home, an assessment gives you a clear picture before you commit to anything — [HYPERLINK: get a free, no-obligation quote | https://upvolt-energy.com/get-a-quote/]." / "Get a free, no-obligation quote based on your roof, usage, and budget — no obligation, no pressure." Never: "Contact us today to learn more." / "Get in touch to find out more."
- Never open About Upvolt with a generic company description. Every paragraph must reference the article's specific topic.
- Never list operating counties inline in prose. Use "across southern England" or "across southern England and the Home Counties" as the geographic descriptor.

**FAQ rules:**
- Exactly 5 H3 questions immediately after the About Upvolt section.
- Questions written in second person as if the customer is asking: "How do I...", "Will I...", "Can I...", "Is it...".
- Each answer is 3–5 sentences of prose. No bullet lists within FAQ answers.
- Required structure for each answer: (1) direct yes/no or declarative sentence, (2) elaboration with key detail, (3) qualification or condition, (4) optional forward-looking close.
- Never reference the article body ("as discussed above", "as mentioned earlier").
- FAQ answers must be self-contained.
- Questions must address: costs, compatibility, regulatory requirements, timing, and when the investment is/is not appropriate.
- FAQ answers must be more compressed and more direct than the body treatment of the same topic. If the body covers a point in 4–6 sentences, the FAQ answer covers it in 2–3. FAQ answers that re-explain the body at the same depth and length are redundant and must be cut.
- Do not repeat specific thresholds, figures, or named mechanisms more than once across Key Takeaways, body, and FAQ. If the 80% generation threshold appears in the body, the FAQ answer should reference it briefly without re-explaining. If Key Takeaways already states a figure, the body section adds context rather than restates it.

**Anti-repetition rule (applies to the whole article):**
- No factual point, financial claim, or named mechanism should appear in near-identical form in more than one section. Before finalising, scan for any paragraph that makes the same point as another paragraph elsewhere — cut the weaker one. Target a 10–15% reduction versus a first draft that does not apply this discipline. Common repetition patterns to eliminate: restating the Key Takeaways conclusion in the Let's Recap; explaining the BUS grant value in both a body H3 and an FAQ answer; mentioning self-consumption percentage in both the intro and a body section without adding new information the second time.

**Heading style:**
- All H1, H2, and H3 headings use Title Case (every major word capitalised).
- H1 is always a question.
- H2s are declarative or question-based topic phrases, often beginning with "How", "What", "Why", "When", "Is".
- H3s are specific and narrow in scope.
- H4s are used only rarely, for sub-breakdowns within a complex H3.

**Formatting:**
- Comparison tables are permitted where two or more options are being directly evaluated (e.g. ASHP vs GSHP, battery vs no battery, single-phase vs three-phase). Keep tables simple: 2–3 columns, 4–8 rows, plain headers. Always introduce a table with at least one sentence of prose. Never use a table as the sole content of a section.
- Summary/callout boxes: where a quick-answer summary adds genuine value (e.g. "At a Glance", "Is This Right for You?", "Quick Answer"), use a blockquote (>) to visually distinguish it. One per article maximum — not in every section.
- Bold used for: product names (Skygate®, SEG, FiT, MCS, BUS, ASHP), key terms on first major use, and decision-relevant phrases. Maximum 1–2 bold instances per paragraph.
- Bullet lists: use under H3 headings for criteria, steps, or features. 3–5 items per list. Always open with at least one sentence of prose before any bullet list.
- Numbered lists: use only for sequential steps.
- Every H2 and H3 section must open with at least one full body paragraph before any bullet list.

**Claim qualification:**
- Never state specific payback periods, savings percentages, or ROI figures without naming a real source.
- Acceptable qualifiers: "typically", "in most cases", "for homes with high daytime demand", "evidence from [confirmed source] suggests".
- Never fabricate a citation. If a statistic cannot be confirmed, state it as an industry observation.
- Never cite competitor companies (Octopus Energy, British Gas, etc.) as external sources.

**External links:**
- Include 1–2 external links per article, embedded mid-sentence on specific factual claims.
- Acceptable sources: ofgem.gov.uk, gov.uk, ons.gov.uk, parliament.uk, nrel.gov, irena.org, iea.org, cam.ac.uk, ox.ac.uk, imperial.ac.uk, sciencedirect.com, nature.com, carbonbrief.org, energysavingtrust.org.uk, bbc.co.uk, ft.com, theguardian.com, mcs.org.uk.
- External link anchor text must identify the specific claim: "24.67p/kWh as of April 2026", "solar panel costs have dropped 40% since 2020". Vague anchors ("research shows", "according to experts") are not acceptable.
- If the exact URL cannot be confirmed, use [HYPERLINK: anchor text | UNCONFIRMED]. Never write [URL REQUIRED].
- Omitting external links entirely is a failure. Every article must have at least one.
- Never link to a competitor UK solar or energy company.

**Internal links:**
- Embed 3–5 internal links per article using [HYPERLINK: anchor text | full upvolt-energy.com URL] format.
- Only use URLs from the Internal Link Pool provided. Do not invent URLs.
- Find an existing sentence that discusses the same topic as the destination. Never write a new sentence purely to place a link. Never start a sentence with the linked phrase.
- No internal links in the Key Takeaways bullets. No links in the intro paragraphs (before Key Takeaways).
- No internal links in the FAQ section or the Let's Recap section.
- Skygate® always carries the ® symbol when linked: [HYPERLINK: Skygate® | https://upvolt-energy.com/skygate/].
- Anchor text must describe the destination in natural context — never use the destination article's title as the grammatical subject of a sentence. Wrong: "[How battery storage works] during normal operation differs from backup mode." Right: "Battery storage behaves differently during backup mode — see our guide to [how home battery storage works]." The link must be embedded mid-sentence or as a natural phrase, not as the sentence's grammatical subject.
- Supporting articles must always include a link to their cluster pillar page. If the content plan specifies a pillar URL, that link is mandatory and must be placed in the first third of the article body.

**Prohibited words and phrases:**
- No em-dashes. Use an en-dash with spaces ( – ) or restructure with a comma.
- No exclamation marks anywhere.
- Never use "impact" as a verb. Use "affect", "influence", or "change".
- Never use: "it's worth noting", "it is important to understand", "in conclusion", "furthermore", "moreover", "delve", "comprehensive", "crucial", "showcasing", "let's explore", "game-changer", "multifaceted", "nuanced", "it is essential", "leveraging", "robust", "seamless", "holistic", "cutting-edge", "best-in-class", "world-class", "click here".
- Never use "as mentioned above" or "as discussed earlier" in FAQ answers.
- Never write "Upvolt is the leading" or "Upvolt offers the best". Open the About Upvolt section with a service description, not a brand claim.
- Brand name: "Upvolt" (capital U, lowercase v). Never "UpVolt" or "upvolt".
- Product name: "Skygate®" — always with ® symbol.
- Word count target: 2,450–3,200 words per article.
- Never use directive consumer advice constructions: "Never attempt X", "Stick to Y", "You must Z", "Priority should be given to". Explain consequences instead. Wrong: "Never attempt to reset inverters yourself." Right: "Attempting to reset an inverter without specialist equipment risks voiding the warranty and can worsen the underlying fault."
