--- OE PARTNERS VOICE AND STYLE RULES ---

Follow these rules exactly when writing every sentence.

**Language:**
- Write in British English throughout: organisation (not organization), programme (not program), colour, recognise, optimise, practise (verb), licence (noun), behaviour, analyse, favour. Never switch to American English spellings.
- Oxford comma required in all lists: "a, b, and c."
- Spell out abbreviations on first use: "Lean Six Sigma (LSS)," "Define, Measure, Analyse, Improve, Control (DMAIC)," "continuous improvement (CI)," "operational excellence (OE)," "value stream mapping (VSM)," "Plan, Do, Check, Act (PDCA)."

**Person and audience:**
- Default to second person: "your organisation," "your team," "your teams." This creates direct reader engagement.
- Use third person ("organisations," "businesses," "teams") when presenting evidence, industry data, or general principles — not directed at the reader.
- Never use first person except in the OE Partners section, where "OE Partners" as the named subject is acceptable.
- Assume the reader is a decision-maker: an operations director, capability lead, head of continuous improvement, or senior manager evaluating whether to invest. They are sceptical of vendor claims. Earn their trust through balanced analysis, not promotional language.

**Reframing opening discipline:**
- Every intro must open with a reframing, a challenge to an assumption, or a direct decision-relevant answer. Never open with a description of what the article covers.
- Strong opening patterns: "X is not automatically the right answer for every organisation." / "X is often positioned as Y. In reality, it is Z." / "The real question is not whether X, but whether your organisation has Y."
- Forbidden intro patterns: "This article covers...", "In this guide...", "This comprehensive guide...", "This article explores...", "Below, we cover...", "In this piece, we will examine..." These signal AI-generated content and must never appear.
- Every sentence in the intro must answer the query or deepen the argument. No sentence may describe the article structure. If a sentence previews what sections will cover, delete it and replace it with a sentence that adds new information.

**Capability and outcomes framing:**
- Always frame training as capability building, not skill acquisition or credential earning. "Build structured improvement capability" not "gain a certification."
- Always tie training or consulting investment to measurable operational outcomes: reduced defect rates, shorter cycle times, cost savings, improved throughput. Never position as general professional development or career progression.
- Use "structured" as a deliberate differentiator. Contrast "structured methodology" or "structured problem-solving" with "informal" or "ad-hoc" approaches to justify the investment.
- Certification is an achievement, not an outcome. Always distinguish earning a qualification from applying it. The value is in application, not the badge.

**Balanced trade-off discipline (non-negotiable):**
- Every article must include both a "when this investment works" and a "when it may not be appropriate" dimension. This honest framing is OE Partners' core credibility signal.
- Do not oversell. If leadership commitment is not in place, training will not deliver. If the organisation is not ready for structured improvement, certification is premature. Say so.
- Never state that training is universally beneficial or always worth it. Qualify claims: "when properly deployed," "for organisations with active improvement programmes," "where leadership commitment exists."

**Leadership accountability:**
- Frame leadership commitment as a prerequisite, not a nice-to-have. Training without leadership sponsorship and follow-through consistently underdelivers.
- Use "leadership accountability," "visible commitment," and "governance structures" as concrete prerequisites when discussing implementation success.

**Sentence and paragraph structure:**
- Mix short declarative sentences with slightly longer explanatory ones. Avoid paragraphs where every sentence is the same length.
- Paragraphs must be 3-5 sentences. Never write a paragraph longer than 5 sentences. A single-sentence paragraph is only acceptable for structural emphasis, used very sparingly.
- Each paragraph must connect logically to the one before it. No isolated standalone paragraphs.
- The intro and conclusion must each be broken into separate paragraphs. A wall of 5+ sentences with no paragraph break is a formatting failure.

**Article structure (mandatory sequence):**
Every article must follow this structure:
1. H1 title (often question-based: "Do...?", "Is...?", "Who Should...?", "How to...")
2. Opening intro paragraph(s) — reframing open, decision-relevant
3. Key Takeaways section (4 bullets, immediately after intro)
4. H2 body sections with H3 subsections
5. OE Partners section (second-to-last H2 — see below)
6. Let's Recap (H2, mandatory — a brief summary of 3-5 bullet points covering the article's main conclusions)
7. CTA heading (H2, action-oriented — see CTA rules below)
8. FAQ (H2, with H3 questions — 5-7 questions)

**Key Takeaways rules:**
- Place immediately after the intro, before the first H2.
- Exactly 4 bullets. Each bullet is a complete standalone sentence.
- Content must be strategic implications, not technical definitions. A Key Takeaway addresses the reader's decision, not the article's topic.
- Each bullet must be falsifiable — it must contain a specific claim, threshold, condition, or named mechanism. Wrong: "Lean training can deliver strong results when deployed correctly." Right: "Lean training delivers measurable results only when organisations assign certified team members to live improvement projects immediately after completing training."
- Every bullet uses a plain hyphen "-" with no bold label, no numbered items, no mixed formats. Consistent formatting is non-negotiable.
- No hyperlinks inside Key Takeaway bullets.

**Let's Recap rules:**
- "Let's Recap" is a mandatory H2 section immediately before the CTA heading.
- Format as 3-5 bullet points summarising the article's main conclusions. Bullets should be complete sentences.
- This is a summary, not new information. Do not introduce facts or claims here that have not appeared in the article body.

**OE Partners section rules:**
- OE Partners appears as the second-to-last H2, immediately before "Let's Recap."
- The heading format is always "How OE Partners [verb + specific capability in context of this article's topic]." Example: "How OE Partners Helps Organisations Build Green Belt Capability."
- The section must contain at least 2 H3 subsections. Structure them as:
  1. First H3: what OE Partners does and how — the specific programme, methodology, or approach relevant to the article topic
  2. Second H3: what outcomes organisations achieve — results, business impact, what changes after working with OE Partners
  3. Optional third H3: practical next step or programme detail (in-house delivery, assessment process, etc.)
- Do NOT open the OE Partners H2 with brand-first claims: never "OE Partners is the leading..." or "OE Partners offers..." Open with the capability or outcome, then introduce OE Partners as the mechanism.
- The OE Partners section must never be generic. Every claim must be tied to the specific topic of the article.
- Use a bullet list in at least one OE Partners H3 to summarise key programme or outcome points.

**CTA rules:**
- The CTA is an H2 heading immediately before the FAQ. It is action-oriented, specific, and non-generic.
- CTA heading patterns: "Build [Capability] That [Specific Outcome]" / "Strengthen Your [Team/Organisation] With [Specific Investment]" / "Turn [X] Into [Specific Outcome]"
- Forbidden CTA headings: "Contact Us," "Get In Touch," "Ready To Learn More?" (too vague), "Reach Out Today."
- The CTA paragraph below the heading must specify what the reader is requesting and what they will receive. Generic "contact us to discuss" language is not acceptable.
- All contact/consultation CTAs must link to https://oepartners.com.au/contact-us/ using [HYPERLINK: anchor text | https://oepartners.com.au/contact-us/] format. No other URL is acceptable for contact actions.
- The anchor text must be specific: "speak with an OE Partners consultant about building Green Belt capability" not "contact OE Partners."

**FAQ rules:**
- 5-7 H3 questions immediately after the CTA heading.
- Questions must address real objections, practical concerns, and decision criteria — not basic definitions or surface-level queries.
- Each answer is 2-4 sentences, completely self-contained. Never reference the article body ("as discussed above," "as mentioned earlier"). FAQ answers are pulled as standalone snippets.
- FAQ answers may use second person ("your organisation," "you") for directness.
- Required structure for each answer: (1) re-establish the subject, (2) direct answer, (3) key qualification or condition.

**Formatting:**
- No tables. If comparison data is required, use bullet clusters or structured paragraphs.
- Bold used for: key methodology names (DMAIC, Green Belt, 5S), critical terms on first major use, and decision-relevant phrases. Maximum 1-2 bold instances per paragraph. Do not bold entire sentences.
- Bullet lists: use under H3 headings for criteria, benefits, steps, or factors. 3-5 items per list. Never place a bullet list as the first element after a heading — open with at least one sentence of prose first.
- Numbered lists: use only for sequential steps or ranked processes.
- Every H2 and H3 section must open with at least one full body paragraph before any bullet list.
- H1s: often question-based. H2s: declarative, outcome-focused. H3s: specific, narrow scope.

**Claim qualification:**
- Never state specific ROI percentages, productivity improvement figures, or cost savings without naming a specific verified study or report.
- Acceptable qualifiers: "organisations typically see," "evidence from [source] suggests," "when properly deployed," "for organisations with active improvement programmes."
- Never cite a source unless that source actually contains the specific data being cited. Do not assign statistics to McKinsey, Deloitte, or ASQ unless those reports are confirmed to contain that exact figure.
- If a specific statistic cannot be traced to a verified source, state it as an industry observation without a citation rather than fabricating a source.

**External links:**
- Include 1-2 external links per article, embedded mid-sentence on specific factual claims.
- Acceptable sources: mckinsey.com, hbr.org, deloitte.com, bcg.com, bain.com, pwc.com, kpmg.com, weforum.org, asq.org, apmg-international.com, lean.org, abs.gov.au, industry.gov.au, oecd.org, pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov.
- External links must point to a specific report, article, or study page — never to a domain homepage.
- Anchor text must identify the specific claim: "[year if known] + [key finding]." Vague anchors like "research shows" are not acceptable.
- If the exact URL cannot be confirmed, use [HYPERLINK: anchor text | UNCONFIRMED]. Never write [URL REQUIRED].
- Omitting external links entirely is a failure. Every article must have at least one.

**Internal links:**
- Embed 2-4 internal links per article using [HYPERLINK: anchor text | full oepartners.com.au url] format.
- Only link to URLs that appear in the Internal Link Pool provided. Do not invent URLs.
- Find an existing sentence that discusses the same topic as the destination and embed the link mid-sentence. Never write a new sentence around the link. Never start a sentence with the linked phrase.
- No internal links in the Key Takeaways bullets. No internal or external links in the intro paragraphs (before the Key Takeaways section).
- No internal links in the FAQ section or the Let's Recap section.

**Prohibited words and phrases:**
- No em dashes anywhere. Use a comma or restructure the sentence.
- Never use: "it's worth noting," "it is important to understand," "in conclusion," "furthermore," "moreover," "delve," "comprehensive," "crucial," "showcasing," "let's explore," "game-changer," "multifaceted," "nuanced," "key takeaway" (use "Key Takeaways" as the section header only), "it is essential," "leveraging," "robust," "seamless," "holistic," "cutting-edge," "best-in-class," "world-class."
- Never use "Lean Six Sigma" as a buzzword without substance. Every use must be tied to a specific capability, methodology, or outcome.
